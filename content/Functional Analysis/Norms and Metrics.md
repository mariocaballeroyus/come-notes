**Definition** (`norm`). Let $V$ be a linear space. A map $|| \cdot ||: V \rightarrow \mathbb{R}, v \mapsto ||v||$ is called a *norm* on $V$ if:

- $||v|| \geq 0$ for all $v \in V$, 
- $||v|| = 0$ if $v = 0$, 
- $||\alpha v|| = |\alpha| ||v||$ for all  $\alpha \in \mathbb{R}$, $v \in V$, 
- $||u + v|| \leq ||u|| + ||v||$ for all $u, v \in V$. 

The last inequality is called *triange inequality*. 

**Definition** (`normed space`). Let $V$ be a linear space with a norm $|| \cdot ||$ defined on it. We call $V$ a *normed space*.

**Examples** (norms on $\mathbb{R}^n$). 
- For $x \in \mathbb{R}^n$ we define the 1-norm:
$$
||x||_1 := \sum_{i=1}^{n} |x_i|
$$
 - For $x \in \mathbb{R}^n$ we define the 2-norm or the Euclidean norm:
$$
||x||_2 := (\sum_{i=1}^{n} (|x_i|)^2)^{1/2}
$$
 - For $x \in \mathbb{R}^n$ we define the $\infty$-norm or maximum norm:
$$
||x||_\infty := \max_{1 \leq i \leq n}{(|x_i|)}
$$
**Examples** (norms on C-Spaces).

- For $f \in C([0,1],\mathbb{R})$ we define the $\infty$-norm or sup-norm:
$$
||f||_\infty := \sup_{x \in [0,1]}{|f(x)|}
$$
- For $f \in C^1([0,1],\mathbb{R})$ we define the $C^1$-norm:
$$
||f||_{C^1} := \sup_{x \in [0,1]}{|f(x)|} + \sup_{x \in [0,1]}{|f'(x)|}
$$
- For $f \in L^2([0,1],\mathbb{R})$ we define the $L^p$-norm:
$$
||f||_{L^p} := \left( \int_0^1{(f(x))^p} \text{d}x \right)^{1/p}
$$
**Theorem** (`natural norm`). Let $V$ be an inner product space. Then$$||v|| := \left< v,v \right>^{1/2} \qquad \text{for all } v \in V$$is a norm on $V$. This norm is called the *natural norm* of the inner product space.

**Theorem** (`Cauchy-Schwarz inequality`). With the understanding that the norm on an inner product space is that generated by the inner product, the *Cauchy-Schwarz inequality* may be written in the alternative form 
$$
| \left< u,v \right> | \leq ||u|| \, ||v|| \qquad \text{for all } u,v \in V
$$
**Theorem** (`parallelogram identity`). Let $V$ be an inner product space. Then the parallelogram identity is valid:
$$
|| u+v ||^2 + || u-v ||^2 \leq 2 \left( ||u||^2 + ||v||^2 \right) \qquad \text{for all } u,v \in V
$$
**Definition** (`metric`). Let $U$ be a set. A map $d: U \times U \rightarrow \mathbb{R}, (u, v) \mapsto d(u, v)$ is called a metric on $U$ if:

- $d(u, v) \geq 0$ and $d(u, v) = 0$ if $u = v$
- $d(u, v) = d(v, u)$
- $d(u, v) \leq d(u, v) + d(w, u)$

for all $u, v, w \in U$. 

**Definition** (`metric space`). A set $U$ with a metric is called a *metric space*.

**Lemma**. Let $V$ be a linear space with norm $|| \cdot ||$. Then $d(u, v) := ||u - v||$ defines a metric on $V$.
